Parlameter Balkans feasibility study

Intro

The following study is a result of a 14-day journey through the Balkans region, with stops in Zagreb, Sarajevo, Podgorica, Tirana, Pri≈°tina, Skopje, and Belgrade. We held workshops with potential local partners in each of the capitals, who also helped us with the creation of this report.

There are many differences among the countries in terms of data availability, data quality and situations with current parliamentary tracking software implementations. However, Parlameter is feasible in all of the countries we visited. Some require more work than others and some might miss a feature or two, but the platform is capable of covering all parliamentary systems included in the study.

Why does this matter?

After three years of active development Parlameter has matured to the point where it can be installed in other countries beside Slovenia. The platform does not use any proprietary technologies and is as such (just like every our other project) completely opensource and available for use.

Moreover, since the development of mySociety's Pombola, which was a very solid attempt of creating an opensource, unified, global solution for parliamentary monitoring appears to be put on hold, parliamentary monitoring software development efforts should be streamlined again. We believe we have successfully built on the foundations that mySociety provided and now wish to make Parlameter the go-to open source solution for parliamentary monitoring.

Replicating Parlameter in several countries will produce network effects. Once a new feature is developed in one of the countries' own Parlameters, every other country can make use of it without additional development costs.

However, in order to gain enough traction in developer communities and get the attention of NGOs working on similar solutions in other countries, we need more countries on board. We believe it would best to start in southeastern Europe, since countries there have similar cultures, parliaments and political systems, and sometimes even languages. Hence the choice of countries, included in the following report.

What's new on Parlameter

Since we last publicly presented Parlameter ongoing development efforts have yielded new features. We present these below, since they are going to be included in any new Parlameter installation.

Parlameter alerts

Clipping from parliamentary sessions! We allow users to subscribe to email notifications (alerts) on chosen trigger-words whenever these are discussed or mentioned in the parliament. The user only needs to tell the app what they want to search for, supply their email and then get reports on debates in the parliament upon mention or on a daily or weekly basis.

Parlatube

Video on the internet is bigger than ever. Not just in terms of gigabytes transferred but also in time spent watching and creating videos online. A lot of parliaments record their sessions, but there are no tools out there to sift through them or search through them via transcripts. Parlatube is a tool that solves those problems and, furthermore, allows users to create clips enhanced with captions and emojis to share on social media or via email. We already tested it in Slovenia with a private TV network and the tool is ready to be installed in countries that publish videos of parliamentary sessions online.

See it in action! ‚Üí

Legislation monitoring

One thing that Parlameter was missing was legislation tracking. We now have an understanding when motions are passed and voting events take place in order to pass a specific piece of legislation. While we previously only displayed voting records, Parlameter now connects these to actual legislation that is passed in the parliament. We also added legislation excerpts for easier understanding of what those passed laws actually change without needing to read through actual bills.

The decision to put legislation at the heart of Parlameter was inspired by Javna Rasprava and will thus also make it easier to integrate with it in BiH & Albania.

Interactive voting records comparator

In politics, strange friendships are sometimes made, even if just for a couple of motions. In Slovenia, we noticed that the biggest coalition party often collaborates with what appears to be its worst public enemy, a radical right wing opposition party, just to get their repressive agenda through even when their own coalition partners do not want to cooperate.

We created an interactive tool that helps understand how and when alliances like that are formed. Users can now query Parlameter voting records database with sentences like ‚ÄúI want to know on which motions party A voted one way, while their membed B and party C voted differently,‚Äù and get real time results.
